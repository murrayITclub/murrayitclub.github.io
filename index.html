Goals: be able to...
  • understand that electricity travels through closed loops called circuits
  • persist through frustration in challenge-based learning
  • reflect on and document learning

Electricity has to travel through a closed loop called a circuit. Here are the circuit-building challenges we tried - hover over the blue text to see solutions!

  1. Light up a lightbulb using a single battery
  2. Light a lightbulb with a battery pack and alligator clip wires See a solution
  3. Use a switch to control the lightbulb See a solution
  4. Build a circuit with a switch, motor, and light: when you flip the switch one way, the light is on and motor is off; when you flip it the other way, the motor is on and the light is off See a solution
  5. Build a circuit with two lights that are both (equally) bright See a solution

What did we learn? This activity shows us that...

  • Electricity has to travel in closed loops called circuits
  • A switch is any device that can open and close a circuit. With switches with more than two legs (the metal connecting pieces), it can be hard to figure out which legs to clip wires to!
  • A circuit is either all on or all off. If you want to have one thing on and one thing off, they each need to have their own path to the battery.
  • If you put more than one light in the same circuit, they'll both be dim. To make them both bright, they each need their own path to the battery. (The first picture is called a series circuit, and the second is a parallel circuit).
